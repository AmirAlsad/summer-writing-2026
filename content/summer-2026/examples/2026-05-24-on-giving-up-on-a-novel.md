I stopped working on the novel in March. I did not decide to stop — I just noticed, at some point in April, that I had not opened the file in six weeks, and that I did not particularly want to. That is usually how it ends with me. Not a decision. A drift.

The novel was about a man who inherits his father's house and finds, inside a wall, a series of letters that were never sent. I know how that sounds. It sounded like that to me too, after a while, which was part of the problem. I had started it because I was genuinely curious about something — about what we keep, and from whom, and whether keeping something from someone is always a kind of cowardice — and somewhere in the second year I lost the thread of the question and was just managing a plot.

What is left is not nothing. There are twenty thousand words I can still read without embarrassment. There is one chapter I think is genuinely good. There is a paragraph near the end that I may use someday in something else, if I can figure out what the something else is.

I used to think giving up was a failure of nerve. I am less sure about that now. Some things you start in order to find out what you actually want to write. The novel was that kind of thing. I know more now than I did. That seems like enough.
