There is a stretch in the middle of *Stoner* — maybe forty pages — that I have read more times than any other piece of prose in my life. I do not mean I love the book. I do love it, but that is not why. I mean that I keep returning to those pages the way you keep returning to a particular argument with a person who is no longer in your life, hoping that this time you will phrase it well enough to win.

The argument, in my case, is about whether a quiet life can be a good one. Williams's whole novel is a sustained, almost cruel insistence that the answer is *yes* — and also, somehow, *no, and also that is the whole point*. Every time I read it, I think I have it. Every time I finish it, the answer slips back behind a door I did not see open.

I do not know what to do with a book that does this to me. I keep it on the shelf next to the kitchen because if I put it in a real bookcase I would lose it for months. I have underlined the same paragraph three times in three different pens. I have not lent it to anyone because I am afraid of what it would say with someone else's marginalia on top of mine.

This is, I think, the closest thing I have to a religious practice — a text I cannot get to the bottom of, that I return to not for answers but to be argued with by something patient. Maybe that is what rereading is for. Not the recovery of a first impression. The slow accrual of a second voice in your head, one you can carry into the rest of the week.
