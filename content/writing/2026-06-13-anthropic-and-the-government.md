Yesterday I wrote a whole piece about how we're all eventually going to route our work through these models. Plain English on one end, the machine on the other, and nothing in between that we'd recognize as ours. I stand by it. The very same day, the U.S. government sent Anthropic a letter and switched off the main power that the entire piece was built around (in the sense that it shows what these models are capable of).

I'm trying to keep today ligh, so let's keep things simple. Anthropic launched Fable 5 and Mythos 5 earlier in the week. On Friday, at 5:21pm, they got an export control directive telling them to cut off access for any foreign national, including their own employees. There's no clean way to enforce that, so they pulled the models for everyone. The reasoning from the federal government was a jailbreak of the model's capabilities and whatnot... You can search up the rest.

Yesterday I said we couldn't be sure we'd ever get off the rollercoaster. Turns out someone can stop the ride whenever they want.

When I first saw the news, I said to my co-founders something along the lines of: "I trust Anthropic more than the government."

Definitely not those words verbatim, because that line sounds ridiculous (and even I can do better than that). There's no way that I can trust a company with that much skin in the game. The sentiment sorta stands though, I just need to clarify it. It sounds like I'm putting Anthropic at a pedestal, when in reality, my expectations of the government are ridiculously low. If I had to put a number rating to it, it would be a -1/10. Not only does the government tend to do a poor job, but it tends to perform the opposite of what it set out to do. I'm being dramatic here, but y'all get the idea.

To be clearer, I trust Anthropic to regulate *themselves* better than I trust the current government to regulate them. I'm not saying Anthropic should be the one making the rules. I'm thinking of that line "if men were angels, no government would be necessary", but this presumes the collective judgement (under a true democracy) or that the judgement of those in power is good enough to control the governed. I don't think that's the case. The authority of the government is legitimate, but having the authority to make a call and actually being informed enough to make it right are two completely different things.

My wariness toward the government here isn't some grand thesis about history, though I may venture to create my own if there wasn't one already. My wariness is merely a result of what I've seen from my one experiences.

I'm telling you I'm more at peace with a private company's judgment than my own government's, about the most powerful tool any of us has ever touched. I don't know how to make that okay. I'm not sure I should.

A lot of people are reading this as less about safety and more about spite. The story goes that it's payback for Anthropic refusing to cooperate with the Department of War on ethical grounds (baller move btw). There's bad blood there. So, when a vague "national-security" letter shows up on a Friday evening and kills their flagship, it's not too difficult to connect the dots.

Do I buy this story of spite? Yeah, mostly. Based on everything I've watched this administration do, a move like this is plausible, unfortunately. At the end of the day, it's just a hunch.

What if it's all 4D chess? Mythos-class models cost a fortune to run, but if the government is the one that makes you turn them off, well then, Anthropic can't be blamed... While they have been asking for regulation, this probably wasn't the output they were expecting.

Who *should* be holding that off switch? Is anyone actually informed enough to be the one holding it? I genuinely don't know yet. I'll have to explore it in a different piece.

Until next time, ✌️.
