People drastically underestimate what they're capable of. But it's not a natural state. It's a symptom of an illness. The illness of being unable to own up to one's mistakes and limitations. The illness of cope. Let me try to develop and communicate this idea.

I used to be a huge fan of David Goggins. I still am in what he stands for, just less active in consuming his content and whatnot. If you don't know him, look him up. Hear his story. He's the prime example that we, humans, are blessed with a remarkable capacity for handling tough matters. A capacity that only reveals itself if we hone it. You can't just show up one day and do 5000 pull-ups. You must first believe it, and then beyond that belief is setting up the necessary, *real* conditions for such a feat (i.e. being strong enough to do many pull-ups).

The ordering is crucial. Having the proper conditions isn't enough to achieve great things. Because the conditions need to be acted upon with effort. And effort is a function of belief. From belief comes the optionality of actually doing the thing. Desire. You might have the conditions and belief of being able to do 5000 pull-ups, but not the desire.

Compare a man that works for his paycheck to a man that works for his fulfillment. In whatever it is they do for work, I guarantee you that the man working for his fulfillment will end (if he isn't already) drastically more successful (in whatever regard) than the man that works for his paycheck. Even if the man that works for his paycheck was struggling to cover rent. I'm tempted to say “even if the lives of his wife and kids depended on it”, but I can't speak to this extreme from my observations. I've heard of some serious reactions in this regime. Let it be known though, that when I say the man works for his fulfillment, I mean that he fulfillment to the full extent. Without his work, he loses his sanity. With that, I'd stand by the claim: You can't classically condition yourself to greatness.

The most systematic process of classically conditioning people, that I can attest to, is the **education system.**

Take a handful of students and ask them why they're in school (separate from the socialization, on which I can share my thoughts through a different piece). Many will say “because I'm forced to.” School functions more like a prison than a place of education for them. After all, if the youth were out and about, their parents would have to tend to them, meaning they wouldn't be contributing to the economy with their labor. Or worse, the youth would bring chaos from their energetic tendencies. Some will answer “to go to university” or “to get a job”. These people are working for the paycheck. A few, if any, will say “to understand/improve the world”. There are so many angles to this last answer, that I'm not going to put effort into enumerating them. As for anyone who truly internalizes the last answer, they're on the path to being the man working for his fulfillment, though there is still much that can throw them off. They must fight to stay on that path, or they risk losing themselves to mediocrity.

The system keeps the focus general, preventing anyone from fully embracing any one thing. Until university, at which point the options are dramatically narrowed to one particular focus. There's a fine line between exposing students to different ideas so that they may develop their interests and actually providing the freedom + resources to dive deep into any topic that interests them. The system understands that there's a right time for focusing on one side versus another (or maybe it doesn't and is instead constructed out of necessity), but it's ridiculously rash in execution.

Though this was mostly a ramble (as opposed to a well-organized piece), there's much to build off of later.
