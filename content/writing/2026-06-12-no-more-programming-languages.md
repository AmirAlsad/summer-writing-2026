## Introduction

A few days ago, Anthropic released their new model, Claude Fable 5. I'm not going to write about the model itself (there's enough of that going around), but about a reaction to it that I haven't been able to get out of my head.

There's this developer named Victor Taelin. If you don't know him, just know he's the founder of Higher Order Company and he's spent years building HVM and Bend, which are his own programming language and runtime. The man literally creates programming languages, so I think it's fair to say that he's one of those "frontier" developers.

He [posted](https://x.com/VictorTaelin/status/2064448425936994742) about his "personal singularity moment". Now I'm not one to pay attention to others' experiences too much, particularly because I won't truly understand until I experience it myself. But, I have to give credit where credit is due, especially with those remarkable people that know much, much more than I do.

Before the release, he was publicly questioning whether the leaked benchmarks were designed to flatter the model. He ran his personal "pet prompts" on the model and it solved them "trivially." He then let Fable 5 loose on his own codebase. The hardest thing he figured he could ask the model to work on. The frontier of programming. In about two hours, it found a speedup of roughly 17x on one of his benchmarks. For context, that's a file that he himself, alongside the previous Claude model and a swarm of GPT agents, could barely make 2x faster. It also fixed a garbage-collection bug that he admits he might've needed days to find, if he ever found it at all. His own words on the experience were that his post might sound like a paid ad, but that he's more concerned than happy...

This stuff isn't necessarily new, but it's getting a lot harder to ignore. When all of these "big players" are talking about something with such concern, it becomes foolish for us commoners to not listen. This isn't some student one-shotting their homework. This is a person who operates at the deepest levels of programming, having his own optimizations beaten by an order of magnitude, on code that he designed the language for.

I'll admit that I'm not well-versed enough to verify these claims myself, but I don't need to be. There's no clear incentive for people like Victor to be saying these things (if anything, it's against their interests), and there are enough of them saying it that I'm concerned. Not necessarily concerned because there's anything inherently wrong in it, but concerned because of how much power lies in it. People relate AI development to the development of nuclear weapons quite a lot, so the analogous understanding is that nuclear technology isn't inherently bad, but damn, there's a lot of power in it. It must be realized though that AI development will affect society at a much larger scale than nuclear weapons, particularly because it's applications are so widespread.

Perhaps this concern of mine is a manifestation of the human aversion to change/uncertainty. It's certainly not calming with the space moving so quickly WITH such high uncertainty. It's kinda like a rollercoaster, in that some people will be having that time of their lives while others are full of fear. The only issue is that we can't be confident that we'll get off this rollercoaster... Anyways, let's continue with the actual piece.

So, the frontier developers aren't the best writers of code anymore. That's established. As it turns out though, this reality cascades down to the programming languages themselves (over time).
Before we get to programming languages though, we need to talk about language in general. Bear with me here, the detour is part of the point that I want to make.


## Human-Spoken Language

The world used to be very divided. People lived in different regions, and that division led people to develop communication in different ways. Each region, each hotspot, developed its own way of speaking as time went on, and as influential people made decisions around language (probably). I'll be upfront that I'm no linguist, so I can't tell you the exact mechanics of how languages form and split, but the rough story shows up even in the basic history we all know. Take Latin for example. Rome falls, the empire fragments, the regions stop talking to each other, and within centuries that one language has split into French, Spanish, Italian, Portuguese, and Romanian. One ancestor, different regions, and five languages whose speakers can't understand each other (for the most part). You can also just look at a map of East Asia. You have people on an island speaking Japanese, and right across the sea there's mainland China and Korea, and right above both of them there's Russia. Four neighbors, separated by nothing more than a sea or a border, who cannot understand a word of each other. Whatever the exact mechanics were, separation left the world full of people who simply could not communicate with each other.

Then, the separation started breaking down. As education spread and technology developed, there's been a sort of globalization effect with language, and now more and more people are learning English (among other languages) alongside their native language. I'm ethnically from Jordan, and I know tons of people in Jordan who speak English. In all reality, there isn't much need for them to. They live in Jordan. They work in Jordan. Their daily lives function perfectly fine in Arabic. But, learning English has become so easy and so powerful because of the network effect. If one person speaks English and other people want to talk to that one person, they'll learn English. As more people learn English, others will also want to learn English to communicate with them. Each new speaker makes the language more valuable for the next. People don't learn English for the sake of knowing English (though there is a sense of prestige associated with it, particularly in places where there's a sense of inferiority to the west). People learn English to access an international audience (and many Jordanians dream of leaving Jordan for opportunity, but that's a discussion for another day).

Now, I want to be careful with something here. It's tempting to think that some languages win out because they're somehow better. Easier, more elegant, whatever. That's just not the case with English. Ask anyone learning it about "though", "through", and "tough". English won because the people speaking it happened to hold the power and the reach, and the network effect took it from there. The network effect doesn't pick the best option. It picks the most influential option and then locks it in. Keep that mechanism in mind...

You see, in the modern, connected world, languages are dying much faster than they're being born. People are getting more connected, not less, and the isolated pockets where new ways of speaking would form just don't exist anymore. Sure, some random guy can create his own language, but it's probably going to be crap, or it's going to look a lot like some other language with some minor changes (again, not a linguist, so I won't pretend to know what officially counts as a "new language", but you get the idea). The conditions for language birth are gone. Divergence requires isolation, and isolation is over.

So, that's the pattern. Isolation breeds divergence, connection breeds convergence, and the network effect crowns a winner that has little to do with merit. Now, that that's clear, let's talk about programming languages (and compare it to human-spoken language).


## Programming Languages

Programming languages also emerged in their own "pockets." Not geographic pockets, but domain pockets. On one side, you have web development. On another side, you have mobile development. On another, GPU programming, systems programming, data science, and so on. And just like with human languages, each pocket developed its own way of communicating. JavaScript for the web. Swift for iPhones. CUDA for GPUs.

You can wonder why all these languages exist in the first place, and as far as I can tell, languages get created for two kinds of reasons (there may be more, but these two cover everything I've come across). The first is expression: making ideas in some domain easier for humans to say. You could take a language designed for one thing and force it to do something else, but it won't be easy to express your ideas in it, so people build notations that fit their domain. The second is reach: making some objective property attainable that humans couldn't reliably attain by hand. And I do mean objective. Languages differ in measurable, provable ways: how fast the code runs, how much memory it eats, what the compiler can guarantee about your program. For example, Rust exists largely because its compiler can mathematically prove that certain memory bugs can't happen. These aren't matters of taste.

Now, both reasons share a single root: compensating for the limits of the human writer.

Think about what a programming language even is, at the bottom of it all. The machine runs binary. If humans could comfortably write binary, or even assembly, we'd have total control over every instruction. But we can't. It's not that assembly is conceptually hard. It's that getting performance out of it is a volume problem: millions of instructions, registers, and memory addresses to track at once, and the human mind simply doesn't have the working memory for it. So, we did something that, in hindsight, should sound familiar. We delegated the low-level writing to a machine. That's what a compiler is. You describe what you want in a higher-level language, and the compiler writes the machine code. Here's the kicker: it writes better machine code than you would. A human with total control loses to a machine with total patience. So a programming language was never really how we write machine code. It's the interface through which we ask a machine to write it for us.

That's one limit compensated for. Volume. The other is trust. Abstractions don't just hide detail, they removes choices. And removing choices is precisely what makes guarantees possible. A guarantee is a proof, and you can only prove things about a program when the space of what could've been written is small enough to reason about. Rust can mathematically promise that certain memory bugs can't happen because it forbids you from writing the patterns that cause them. The silly mistake isn't caught. It's prevented.

That's the beauty of it. By restricting the human, the language protects them from their own mistakes, and that very same restriction is what gives the compiler the basis for proving and optimizing. Either way, notice who the design is built around. Humans. The language is a guardrail, and guardrails are built around the driver.

One must ask the critical question. Who's behind all this? Who's actually building these programming languages? They don't just appear. There has to be some person or group of people behind it, on the front lines, when a new language is given life. From what I've seen, the story almost always starts the same way. Someone working at the frontier of a domain runs into one of those two walls (either it can't be said cleanly or can't be done reliably) and decides the fix is a new language. This is exactly the kind of person Victor is. His Bend exists so that you can write normal-looking code and have it automatically run across thousands of GPU cores, something no human can reliably wire up by hand.

I'll admit my view of who builds languages was a bit outdated, according to my research. I had pictured the lone developer (or small team) as the main character, and that was true for a long time (C, Python, and Ruby all started this way). But the dominant languages of the last fifteen or so years are corporate products. Go is Google. Swift is Apple. TypeScript is Microsoft. Rust was incubated at Mozilla. The corporations took over language creation a while ago, mostly because they were the ones operating at a scale where hitting those walls hurt the most (and they could afford to pay people to solve it properly).

It's important to notice what didn't change when the corporations took over. Every one of those languages was still written for human developers. Open-sourced. Readable. Documented to death. Designed to be learned. A language is worthless without people writing things in it, so every language, corporate or not, had to win the heart of the people. Whether the motivation was ease of expression or better reach, the entire existence of a programming language has been justified by the humans on the receiving end.

All of these languages, at the end of the day, compile down to the same thing: instructions the machine can execute. In fact, a lot of today's languages literally share compiler infrastructure. That means the diversity of programming languages lives almost entirely at the human-facing surface. Below the surface, the languages have already converged. The variety exists for us, the humans.


## The Death of Programming Languages

So, we've established that programming languages get born when frontier developers hit one of two walls, and that every language ever made was designed around the limits of the human writer. Now, let's revisit our new reality. The frontier developers aren't writing the code anymore.

Remember how I said the compiler story should sound familiar? We delegated the writing of machine code to compilers because humans couldn't handle the volume. What's happening right now is the same move, just one layer up. We're delegating the writing of the high-level code itself to AI coding agents because there's so much demand for code. And just like the compiler ended up writing better machine code than the human, the model is starting to write better high-level code than the human. Victor's 17x speedup wasn't the model being a faster typist. It was the model being a better writer.

This new reality kills new languages through two separate mechanisms (as far as I've identified), so let's explore each of them.

The first is the death of the itch. If the frontline developers aren't the ones writing the code anymore, there's no incentive for them to put in the work and effort that goes behind creating a new language. Think about it through the two walls. The expression wall ("this is painful to say in existing languages") only hurts if you're the one doing the saying. The AI doesn't complain about verbosity. It doesn't get annoyed writing the same boilerplate for the ten-thousandth time. It would be a real pain, and also weirdly funny, if it did. "Nooooo, I don't want to code another B2B SaaS dashboard 😭😭😭"

Getting back to the point, the friction that has historically motivated every new language is felt by the writer, and the writer is no longer human. A new language is a solution to a pain that's disappearing. Because of that, I really just don't see why people would go through the effort (unless its represented as an artistic endeavor, but I doubt that possibility).

The second mechanism by which programming languages are killed is a bit more complex, and to explain it, I need to give some quick context on how the underlying models work. An AI model learns to write code the same way it learns to write English. Lots and lots of data. Mountains of it. The model has seen essentially all the public code ever written, and its competence in a language is roughly proportional to how much of that language it has seen. That's why these models are at their best with Python and JavaScript (the internet is drowning in both).

Now, to be fair, they're not helpless outside the popular languages. These models generalize impressively, and we saw that with Victor (granted, the machinery behind a niche language is usually built in mainstream languages anyways). But, there's a real gap between "competent" and "master," and the gap is in reliability. In a language the model has barely seen, it makes more subtle mistakes and knows fewer of the tricks that only come from having seen a million real-world examples. Think of a brilliant polyglot who studied your language from a textbook last week versus a native speaker.

This gap (between "competent" and "master") doesn't need to be big. It just needs to exist. Put yourself in the adopter's seat, choosing a language for something that matters. The entire premise of this new era is that you're not reviewing every line anymore. You're trusting the writer. So, do you pick the language where the writer is battle-tested across billions of lines, or the one where it's "competent, probably, mostly"? Exactly. And every time someone makes that choice, they're feeding more code into the pile that makes the next model even better at the incumbent language. A small gap, compounded by the network effect, becomes a permanent one. We literally just covered this. English didn't need to be the better language. It needed a head start and a network effect.

And suppose the gap didn't even exist. Suppose the model could write some brilliant new language perfectly from day one. Why would anyone switch? The selling points of a new language were always aimed at the human writer, and the human isn't writing anymore. For a new language to be worth anything now, the model would have to be objectively better through it. Faster code, fewer bugs, less compute. We'll explore this concept a bit more later on...

Now, let's fast forward to when everyone is routing their work through AI. Say some brilliant developer ignores mechanism one, puts in the years, and creates a genuinely better language (by the same objective standards we mentioned earlier). It doesn't matter. The AI can't write it well, because there's no corpus. And since developers now produce code through the AI, a language the AI is bad at is dead on arrival. Nobody adopts it, so no corpus ever forms, so the AI stays bad at it. Meanwhile, every line of AI-generated Python adds to the pile that makes the next model even better at Python.

This mechanism should sound familiar. It's the English network effect, just with a ratchet attached. If one person speaks English and others want to talk to them, they learn English. If the AI speaks Python and you want to build through the AI, you use Python. Each new speaker makes the language more valuable for the next. The network effect picks a winner (one that, again, has little to do with merit) and locks it in. Except this lock-in is far more brutal than anything human languages faced. A human can choose to learn a beautiful new language out of curiosity or passion. The model's abilities are fixed at training time, by whoever decides what goes into the training data. Which raises a question that I find equally obvious and unsettling: who decides what goes into the training data?


## Enter the Labs

The answer is obvious. The companies training the models. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and the handful of others in the running. They decide what code their models see, which means they decide what languages their models are good at. That alone is a staggering amount of power to sit in so few hands... but it isn't even the most fright-inducing part.

I just spent a whole section arguing that a new language dies because there's no corpus for the AI to learn from. That's true for you and me. It is not true for the labs. Because the labs don't have to find a corpus. They can make one.

To see why that's possible, I need to tell you about a moment that I think about every so often. Back in 2016, Google's AlphaGo beat one of the best Go players in the world. Impressive, but it had studied mountains of human games to get there. The part that should give you chills came a year later, with its successor, AlphaZero. They threw out the human games entirely. AlphaZero was given the rules and nothing else, then made to play against itself, millions of times, learning purely from which moves won. No human knowledge at all. And it didn't just match the human-trained version. It absolutely dominated it, and invented strategies that thousands of years of human players had never found.

Why am I telling you this? Because of why it worked. Self-play only works when you can objectively grade the outcome. Go has a win condition. You don't need a human to tell you who won, the board tells you. So the machine could generate its own data, grade itself, and bootstrap from zero.

Now think back to that "objective" property of code I made you sit with earlier. Does it compile? Do the tests pass? How fast does it run? How much memory does it use? Every one of those is checkable by a machine, no human judgment required. Which means code is exactly the kind of thing you can run self-play on. A lab could invent a brand-new language tomorrow, with zero humans having ever written a line of it, then have the model write billions of programs in it, keep the ones that compile and pass and run fast, and iterate. The agent just learns it. The cold-start problem that strangles your new language in the crib (no corpus, so the AI is bad at it, so nobody uses it, so no corpus) simply does not apply to the one entity that can manufacture the corpus out of thin air.

Now, as we've discussed, for a new language to be worth anything, the model would have to be objectively better through it. The same way AlphaZero found Go strategies no human ever did, a model doing self-play in a fresh language could find ways of expressing computation that are faster, safer, leaner than anything we've built, precisely because it isn't constrained by what reads nicely to a human. We optimized our languages for us. A lab can optimize a language for the machine.

So, let's follow that line of thought to its conclusion. You end up with a company (let's say Anthropic, since Fable 5 is what started this whole spiral) that authors the language, authors all the code written in it, and is the only fluent reader of it. The loop closes completely. A programming language from Anthropic that no human developer ever needs to learn. Maybe one that no human developer can learn.

Notice what's actually lost there. It isn't human authorship. We already gave that up. What's lost is human auditability. Right now, when a corporation ships a language, it's open, readable, documented. Anyone can crack it open and check the code. In the world I'm describing, nobody outside the loop can read the language, let alone verify what's been written in it. And this isn't just my paranoia talking. It's a live worry among the people building these systems. There's real concern in AI safety circles about models drifting toward ways of reasoning and communicating that humans can't follow, because legibility is one of the only tools we have to keep an eye on these things. An AI-native programming language is that exact fear.

Do I think the researchers at these AI labs would go this far? Certainly not in the near future. But an AI-native programming language certainly has its benefits. And there's nothing stopping these frontier labs from taking this approach in the future (at this rate, I doubt any legislation is going to come).

Now, if no human writes it and no human reads it... is it still a language at all? I wouldn't say so. A language, the way we actually use the word, implies a community of speakers. It's something shared, something between people. We don't call the noises a whale makes a language, or a dog's barking, even though there's clearly information moving around in there. It's communication, sure, but it isn't a language, because we're not part of the conversation. That bottom-layer substrate is the same. It's the most precise, most powerful "communication" ever constructed, and it has an audience of exactly one kind of entity, none of which is us.


## So, What's Left?

If new languages can't be born, and the old ones are just legacy corpus the models happen to be good at, then where does this actually settle? Is it Python forever? Does one of the lab's machine-languages win and become the one true tongue?

Let's take things back a bit. Why do we have so many programming languages in the first place, instead of one giant language that does everything? Part of it is genuine machine differences (a GPU fundamentally computes differently than a CPU). But a much bigger part of it is, again, us. A language that did everything would be enormous, and no human could hold all of it in their head at once. So we shard it. We carve the world of programming into domains, and each domain gets a language small enough for a human to actually learn and wield. The fragmentation isn't really about the machines. It's about our working memory, the same bottleneck from before, showing up yet again.

When you pull the human out of the middle, the single biggest force pushing toward many languages, namely that no one mind can hold one universal language, just disappears. The machine doesn't need the world chopped into bite-sized domains. It doesn't get overwhelmed. It can hold the whole thing at once. The reason for the variety evaporates, and what's left underneath was always heading toward one thing anyway (remember, below the surface, the languages had already converged).

Think about what a programming language has always been: a bridge. A compromise structure sitting in the middle, human-readable enough for us on one end, precise enough for the machine on the other. It exists to connect two parties. But if the human steps out of the writing and the reading, you don't need a better bridge. You don't need a bridge at all. The two ends collapse toward each other, and the whole middle layer, the thing we've been calling a programming language for however many decades, just falls out.

So, what's actually left is two things, and neither one is a programming language.

On top, there's English. Or whatever human language you happen to think in. This is the part I find kind of beautiful, in a full-circle way. Remember the start of this piece, the whole story about English winning through network effects? It turns out English wasn't just winning the race among human languages. It was quietly winning the race among all languages, programming ones included. The way you "program" becomes describing what you want, in plain human language, to a model. The spec. The intent. The conversation. That's the only layer a human ever touches.

On the bottom, there's whatever the machine emits to talk to itself and to other machines. The self-play substrate from the last section. To be clear, there's nothing magical or forbidden about this universal compute "language." It's just symbols and rules, like anything else. A determined human could sit down and learn it, in theory at least. The reason no one will isn't that it's beyond us. It's that it isn't for us, and it isn't shaped by our limits.

Every language we've ever made was bent around that working-memory bottleneck. We invented function names and modules and clean abstractions precisely so a small mind could chunk a big problem and never have to see the whole thing at once. A machine with an enormous context window doesn't need any of that. So the substrate it converges on might not be "advanced" so much as flat, with very few of the abstractions that exist purely to coddle human memory, more explicit, more repetitive, optimized for machine verification rather than human comprehension. Learnable in principle, useless in practice, like sitting down to read a phone book cover to cover.

So the title of this piece turns out to be more literal than I meant it when I scribbled it down. It isn't that one language wins, or that programming gets sad and boring. It's that the programming language, as a category, was scaffolding. Scaffolding built around a human standing in the middle of the process. Pull the human out, and the scaffolding has nothing left to hold up. No more programming languages. Just us talking on one end, the machines humming on the other, and nothing in between that we'd recognize as ours.
