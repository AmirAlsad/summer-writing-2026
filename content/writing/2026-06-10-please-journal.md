If anyone were to ask me for my best advice, advising that they journal would be of the essential pieces.

It does wonders for the mind that finds itself full of half-assed thoughts.

When I say journal, I don't mean to write about one's day. I suppose that's one way to go about it, but only because it satisfies a deeper understanding. To reflect on one's thoughts and to do so in a manner that forces one to confront their underlying assumptions and biases.

Writing is the most fundamental way of fulfilling the second portion (of the deeper understanding), though it really boils down to the person and their capacity to be consistent in thought. Consider speaking as an alternative. It's very easy, at least for me, to hide behind a veil of ambiguity in the words that I speak. Not consciously. Simply a result of the language that I'm accustomed to using verbally (which is worth distinguishing from language we use while writing). A thought experiment can easily showcase what I'm saying. Think of your average, lying politician. It's pretty easy to imagine how they can get away with a speech full of nonsense. A speech that revolves around emotionally inciting the audience. Contrast that with the case in which the same speech is written down on a sheet of paper and disperesed to the same audience. If the politician was BS-ing, as they usually do, it'll be much easier (by a lot) to take notice because you can re-read the same lines and cross-reference statements or lines of reasoning across paragraphs.

The same happens with writing. When you write something down, you either commit to it or you don't. You can't commit to it at one point and change your mind seconds later. Because the thought exists "on paper". It's documented in a manner that can't be disputed.

I'm thinking I might dive deeper into this topic. Expploring some of the research and other views on journaling. Perhaps even figuring out how AI affects it.
